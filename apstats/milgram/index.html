The Milgram Experiment

Is Obedience Universal?

Created by Brian Bakerman and Rudhra Raveendran

The Experiment

The experiment consisted of having volunteers play the role of a "teacher," and have to ask questions to a "learner." The learner would always be an actor, and would fake screams and pain when given a fake shock. The shock was administered by the teacher whenever the learner answered incorrectly to a question, and the voltage increased with each missed question.

Also note that the teachers could stop the experiment at any time if they said they did not want to continue 4 times.

Who

40 male participants between the ages of 20 and 50 in the New Haven Area, who ranged from unskilled workers to professionals.

What

The willingness of participants to shock "learners," and follow orders.

When

The experiments began in July 1961, a year after the trial of Adolf Eichmann, but the procedure itself was carried out in 1963. Variations on the experiment were carried out afterwards.

Where

Two rooms in the Yale Interaction Laboratory in Yale University. One for the learner, one for the teacher and experimenter.

Why

To answer Milgram's question:

"Could it be that Eichmann and his million accomplices in the Holocaust were just following orders? Could we call them all accomplices?"

How

Participants were told to ask the learners questions, and if the learners answered wrong, they were shocked. With each subsequent incorrect answer, the voltage of the shock would be increased.

Experiment Objective

To see if authority affected the level of obedience in an individual, and if the Nazis who carried out the Holocaust could've been following orders and not acting of their own accord.

Type of Study

The Milgram Experiments were non-randomized, single-blind experiments that always put participants in the teacher position to see how they responded to their instructions. While the participants were told that the positions of learner and teacher were randomized, it was no so.
